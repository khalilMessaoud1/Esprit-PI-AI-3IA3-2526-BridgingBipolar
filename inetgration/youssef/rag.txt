Bipolar Disorder Knowledge Base
1. Overview and Characteristics
•	Bipolar disorder is a chronic or episodic mental health illness characterized by alternating mood episodes. 
•	The primary types of episodes include mania, hypomania, depression, and mixed episodes. 
•	These episodes involve extreme changes in mood, energy, activity, and behavior. 
•	A typical episode lasts at least one to two weeks, with symptoms present most of the day, nearly every day. 
•	Between episodes, individuals may experience stable periods where their mood returns to a baseline. 
•	Without proper treatment, mood episodes can become more frequent over time. 
2. Types of Mood Episodes
•	Mania: A severe episode defined by an abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood coupled with increased energy and activity. It lasts at least 7 days, or any duration if hospitalization is required. Symptoms include inflated self-esteem or grandiosity, a decreased need for sleep, rapid speech, flight of ideas, distractibility, and increased goal-directed activity. Mania is highly associated with impulsivity and risky behaviors such as excessive spending, unsafe sex, and risky investments. It causes significant impairment and may feature psychosis. 
•	Hypomania: This episode shares the exact same symptoms as mania but is less severe. It must last for at least 4 consecutive days. The defining differences are that hypomania does not cause severe functional impairment, does not require hospitalization, and does not include psychosis. Because of this, it often goes unnoticed. 
•	Major Depressive Episode: This episode requires at least 5 symptoms during the same period and causes significant distress or functional impairment. Core symptoms include a persistent low, sad, or hopeless mood, alongside a loss of interest or pleasure in activities. Additional symptoms involve sleep disturbances, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness or guilt, poor concentration, appetite or weight changes, psychomotor changes, and recurrent suicidal thoughts. 
•	Mixed Episodes: These occur when symptoms of mania and depression happen simultaneously, leading to rapid mood changes and emotional instability. An example is experiencing a depressed episode alongside an elevated mood, grandiosity, reduced sleep need, and risky behavior. 
3. Diagnosis Classifications
•	Bipolar I Disorder: Diagnosis requires at least one manic episode, which may require hospitalization. Depressive episodes often occur but are not strictly required for this diagnosis. 
•	Bipolar II Disorder: Requires a history of at least one hypomanic episode and at least one major depressive episode. A history of a full manic episode rules out Bipolar II. 
•	Cyclothymic Disorder: Features recurrent hypomanic and depressive symptoms representing long-term mood instability, but the symptoms are less severe and do not meet the full criteria for distinct episodes. 
•	Specifiers and Patterns: Bipolar disorder can include specific specifiers like rapid cycling (four or more episodes in a year), seasonal pattern, mixed features, anxious distress, melancholic features, or atypical features. 
4. Causes, Triggers, and Comorbidities
•	Biological and Genetic Causes: Bipolar disorder is linked to differences in brain structure, function, and neurotransmitter chemistry. It also has a strong genetic component, running in families due to multiple genes rather than a single responsible gene. 
•	Triggers: Episodes can be triggered by stress, sleep deprivation, major life changes, and childhood trauma. Additional triggers include disruptions in daily routine, substance use, and certain medication side effects. 
•	Comorbidities: The disorder frequently co-occurs with anxiety disorders, ADHD, substance use disorders, and eating disorders. 
5. Diagnosis Protocols and Risks
•	Diagnosis is made by a psychiatrist based on symptom severity, duration, episode frequency, family history, and the impact on daily functioning. 
•	Misdiagnosis is common, as it can be confused with schizophrenia or thyroid issues. 
•	When psychotic features are present, the delusions or hallucinations match the individual's mood, meaning grandiosity occurs during mania and guilt or hopelessness occurs during depression. 
•	Urgent Referrals: Immediate specialist referral is necessary in cases of mania, severe depression, mixed episodes, suicide risk, or if the patient poses a danger to themselves or others. 
•	Key Risks: Patients are at risk for suicide, self-neglect, substance abuse, and mania-associated harms like accidents or neglecting family. 
6. Treatment and Interventions
•	Mood Stabilizers: Lithium is used to treat mania, prevent relapse, and reduce suicide risk, though it requires blood, kidney, and thyroid monitoring every 3 months. Anticonvulsants like Valproate treat mania, and Lamotrigine is used to treat bipolar depression and prevent relapse. Both Valproate and Lithium carry significant risks during pregnancy and mandate specialist intervention. 
•	Antipsychotics: Medications such as Olanzapine, Quetiapine, and Risperidone are prescribed for treating mania, providing mood stabilization, and managing psychotic symptoms. 
•	Antidepressant Rules: Antidepressants must be used with extreme caution because they can trigger a mania switch; therefore, they must be combined with a mood stabilizer and should generally be avoided for long-term use. 
•	Psychological Therapies: Effective therapeutic interventions include Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Interpersonal Therapy, psychoeducation, and family-focused therapy. 
•	Alternative Treatments: Severe or resistant cases may require Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT), Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS), or Light therapy for seasonal patterns. 
7. Long-Term Management
•	Episode Prevention: Prophylactic treatment (like Lithium, Olanzapine, or Quetiapine) is initiated if a patient has a severe episode or multiple episodes, and should be continued for at least two years. 
•	Health Monitoring: General monitoring for patients on these medications should regularly check weight, blood pressure, glucose, and lipids. 
•	Lifestyle Management: Crucial long-term management advice includes ensuring strict medication adherence, maintaining a regular sleep routine, avoiding stress and overwork, and monitoring for early signs of relapse.
